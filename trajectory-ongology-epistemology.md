# Trajectory ontology: Epistemological bases and contemplations 

In this file I try to clarify my current epistemological bases.

## Experiential bases

1. Direct experiences here and now constitute the most certain knowledge we have.  
   As soon as our mental system starts to interpret the experience, the resulting knowledge is less certain.

    Here I differentiate between the direct experience and any (layered) interpretations of the experience.
    My best understanding is that it is hard to experience without interpretation, due to our mental conditioning and habits. 
    Below, when I define OSC, ASC and "magick", I'm classifying direct experiences by their interpretation,
    in order to bring the underlying direct experiences to the same bases.

2. Direct experiences can be divided into a) external or (direct) sensory experiences and b) internal or remembering / thought experiences
    - This is quite similar to Shinzen Young's division
    - One possible finer detail may be John Yates' "moment-by-moment" model, but at this point I don't have direct experience of that, 
      but  I have a friend (Petri) who does claim having first-person experiences of Yates' model.

3. The nature of direct experiences (both external and internal) can be studied with meditation
    - One approach is to cultivate concentration, clarity and equanimity (cf. Shinzen Young)

4. Direct experiences that our system interprets as altered or "deeper" states of consciousness (ASC) belong to our direct experiences.
    - Epistemologically they have equal bases and relevance as our experiences in the ordinary states of consciousness (OSC)
    - While affectionally appealing, whether they have ontologically equal bases remains as an open question; cf. #5 below.

4.1 Direct experiences that our system interprets as precognition, healing "energy", extraordinary synchronic events, 
    or other "magick", also have, as direct experiences, equal epistemic bases to OSC and ASC.

5. Our direct experiences, whether in OSC, ASC, or "magick", 
   cannot be directly interpreted as telling anything about the underlying essence of being, or the ontology.

Corollary 1:  All interpretations of our experiences, including all ontological claims, 
              are on a lesser epistemic basis than direct experiences. 

    This is important to keep in mind when 
      a) going into any ontological claims, and when 
      b) considering anything that we measure, as measurement itself is an interpretation of direct experiences.

6. Our system always tends to interpret our direct experiences, in a layered manner.

    Meditation experiences gained by cultivating concentration and clarity, by allowing us to cultivate recursive experience, 
    i.e. by allowing us to focus our mental experiental apparatus on the process of interpreting experiences,
    seems to indicate that our system automatically applies a multi-layer interpretation to our experiences.
    In OSC, we usually don't notice this at all, unless we've developed our mental clarity.

    Later on, we will approach this from cognitive science, contemplative science, and Theravada (papañca) points of view.

7. There are various models of how the layered interpretation takes place, with from about five to several dozens of layers / faculties.

    The interpretation seems to take place in a somewhat iterative and recursive manner.

    In the literature we can identify roughly five gross layers, which may be first divided into two rough layers: 
     a) automatic interpretation and 
     b) culturally affected interpretation.
    
    The automatic interpretation seems consist of 
      1) identifying immediate affective response (unpleasant/neutral/pleasant), and
      2) identifying objects (e.g. a piece of coloured sheet of papery material) and concepts (e.g. "maybe a banknote?")
    
    The culturally affected interpretation seems to consist of
      3) composing of independent existence of objects ("There is a piece of coloured paper")
      4) composing of a subjective experience ("There is a me seeing this piece of paper")
      5) integrating to narrative interpretation ("I am getting a 10 euro banknote")

    It shall be noted that the identification of objects and especially concepts is iterative/recursive and 
    strongly culturally conditioned.  Within a conditioned mind, there seems to be a preliminary recognition
    of objects and concepts before establishing the indepenent existence of objects or the self, but the
    identity of the objects and concepts appear to be confirmed or re-cognized only later on, within the cultural narrative.

8. With remembering / thinking, there seems to be a quite similar layered interpretation process going on.

    Apparently this process has been somewhat less studied in its the fine-grained phenomenological structure 
    than the interpretation of sensory experiences, especially by science but also by Buddhist meditators.  
    However, the following can be tentatively stated.

    There seems to be a clear difference between remembering and thinking.
    In remembering, the memory images are associated with a historical-contextual anchor.
    Thinking creates that anchor implicitly by being directly attributed to the current self as its generator.
    When pondering the memories and thoughts of others, this anchor gets more complicated.

    With this, we can posit a structure quite similar to the interpretation of sensory experiences:

    Automatic intepretation:
      1) affective response (pleasant/neutral/unpleasant) — same as sensory, but applied to internally generated material
      2) pattern construction — fragmentary traces are re-constructed into a coherent images, scenes, or propositions

    Culturally conditioned:
      3) ontological belief attribution — "this is a real memory / this is a true thought" 
      4) source/authorship attribution — locating the context of the memory ("I remember this from...") or thought ("I think that ...")
      5) narrative integration — fitting the content into the ongoing autobiographical story and belief system; same as above

    Herenin the overall structure is same as in #7: 1) good/neutral/bad, 2) patterns (objects/concepts), 3) independent existence,
    4) adding/composing the subjective, me-centered point of view, 5) integration to the ongoing self-narrative.

9. In ASC, depending on the "depth" of the state, especially the culturally conditioned layers change their nature.

    In most ASC, the narrative aspect weakens or even completely disappears.  
    What there is here and now becomes more important than what is happening or what has been planned.
    These experiences are quite common and e.g. typical already in Csikszentmihalyi's flow states.

    When approaching basic samadhi or rigpa, the generated sense of self, or subjective view, weakens or disappears.
    There is no longer any self experiencing, there is just the experience without any experiencer.
    This I have experienced myself [sic].

    In some ASCs, the boundaries between objects and concepts start to disappear or merge. 
    This is maybe most typical in psychedelic experiences, where it may take place without the narrative or subjectivity disappearing.
    There the object boundaries may dissolve with narrative and self intact, but more commonly all three dissolve together.

    In ASC, also the other layers change their nature, such as affective responses heightning, 
    becoming refined or distored, or fading (e.g. jhānas beyond the first one). 
    With psychedelics, the forming of objects and concepts may become largely distorted,
    leading to experience such as time loops or complete dissolution with external substance.
    However, these changes are not so important for our phenomenological considerations and we largely ignore these.

10. To understand "magick", we need togo deeper into the construction of the self-narrative.

    In our culture, we usually generate a narrative that is based the following underlying assumptions:

      a) Time is linear.  Events follow each other.
      b) Events are caused by other events.  Each experienced event can be understood as a consequence of prior events, its causes.
      c) Causation requires spatial or informational locality.  We don't expect "spooky action by distance" or caused by "magick."
      d) Perceived coincidences are not meaningful, but just results of random processes.
      e) Our mental states are private and separate from the rest of the world.

    "Magick" experiences seemingly violate one or more of these expectations.
    It is noteworthy that some "magick" experiences are quite common, 
    e.g. about 25% of the population seems to have some experiences that they experience as precognition, during their life.

    We return to the role of "magick" later on. 
    At this point we merely note that they do exist, as reported experiences.  
    I have my personal share of them, and therefore I take them, as experiences, seriously.

-------

At this point I consider it clear that direct experience is our most certain source of knowledge.
However, it is hard to be sure even of any embodied knowledge originating from direct experience,
due to the interpretive appratus that automatically runs in our system.
This goes so deep that it becomes hard to define, exactly, what we mean with direct experience.
Furthermore, our interpretive apparatus may be miscalibrated and biased, and apparently is, 
due to both prior evolutionary pressure (automatic lower layers) and cultural conditioning (higher layers),
and possibly due to other factors — e.g. "choosing" a trajectories (but this goes deep and we are not ready for that yet.)

Hence, in order to make any progress, we need to make assumptions.

Depending on the assumptions we make, we will end up with very different ontological models,
spanning from solipsism (and certain left-hand-path interpretations) to Kastrup style dissociation models.

Clearly each set of assumptions needs to be (somewhat) coherent, meaning that it needs to be 
internally consistent (enough) and give a reasonably good explanation to the variety of direct experiences
that we have experienced ourselves or that we expect to be "true" based on literature and other reports, 
i.e. experiences reported credibly and independently by multiple people across different contexts and traditions, 
forming stable inter-subjective patterns. (But even this requires first those assumptions...)

## First assumptions: there are other subjects with sufficiently similar experiential world

At this point we apparently need to make first one minimal ontological assumption and then some methodological ones.
(We will later try to alleviate our reliance on these, but it is easier first to develop with these assumptions.)

First, I choose to assume that other subjective minds exist, thereby rejecting e.g. pure solipsism.

As far as I can see, this is an ontological assumption. 
We could also develop a coherent ontology by assuming that everything,
including the existence of other people (and ourselves) is just e.g. divine play (līlā) by Shiva,
but such an assumption would make it hard to drill into the apparent structure of our experience.
That is, while it may well be that everything is just līlā, this particual flavour of līlā
seems to have structural constraints that we want to map and understand, at least to some extent.

This assumption allows us to consider the phenomenological patterns that — given this view — 
appear robustly across independent observers, in different cultures, different traditions, and different times.
These patterns include both the appearance of the "ordinary" material world but also the reported
experiences of the dissolution of self in deep concentration, the dissolution of object-boundaries in specific states, 
the structures of insight experiences, the phenomenology of the "gone moment" (Shinzen) or the recognition described as rigpa,
as well as the less well but still documented experiences of past life memories, near death experiences, precognition, and so on.

The convergence of these independent reports constrains what further ontological models are permissible. 
A model that cannot account for these inter-subjective patterns is epistemically worse than one that can, all else being equal.

### Alleviating cultural conditioning

When proceeding with out assumption of other people actually existing (at least in some sense) and
them experiencing the world sufficiently similar to us so that we can (to some extent) rely on their reports,
we have to carefully analyse the caveats created by the interpretation processes discussed in points #6–10 above.

That is, based on my direct epistemic understanding, derived from my own experiences (though admitedly
interpreted in a cultural context), I have no option but always take everyone's descriptions 
of their experiences as culturally conditioned.

In other words, we augment our minimal assumption of other minds existing with the assumption
that each of the minds — both our own and those of others — is necessarily culturally conditioned.

Hence, in order to draw bases for further ontological assumptions (and to handle our initial assumption(s) with more care),
we need to defined rules or methods on how to recognise cultural assumptions as such and how to shed them.

Given our primacy of direct experience, clearly our preferred method is direct experience, e.g. through meditation.
These we call the first-hand experiences.
We give that higher epistemic stance compared to hearing from others, or reading from books, noting that even 
here the caveat of cultural conditioning does apply, especially when describing our direct experiences to others.

Other potential sources include the following:

1. Direct first hand experiences described by advanced practitioners, in the form of 
1.1. so-called transmissions and
1.2. (personal) teachings.

The apparent difference between 1.1 and 1.2 is that 1.1 includes an affective channel that makes the teaching feel more real.
(But again, the affectively positive aspect of transmission does not necessarily mean that it is more real than something else.)
These we call, collectively, second-hand experiences.

It is noteworthy that some traditions describe transmission as (partially) bypassing cultural conditioning,
thereby making it easier to adopt less-culturally-conditioned or differently culturally conditioned structures.
At this point, we remain open to such a possibility, but due to relatively little experience of receiving transmission, not commit to this.

2. Apparently similar experiences described in multiple cultures.

These we call third-hand experiences.
Herein, a first issue is how to recognize and classify the similarities and differences.
Then, a second issue is how to select those combined reports that appear most adjacent to first-hand and second-hand experiences.
I deliberately do not go into the details of these here, just noting these as difficulties that need to be addressed.

An advantage of the third-hand experiences of the first-hand and second-hand is that — at best — they are carefully corroborated and curated.
Therefore the best of them are typically very good sources for initially noticing cultural conditioning. 

However, given my preference on eventually relying on first-hand experiences, preferably ASC with high clarity, 
I choose to use third-hand experience reports mostly as 
  a) guides towards first-hand experiences and 
  b) vocabulary to describe first-hand experiences,
while being fully aware that using them as guides will bias the interpretation of the first-hand experiences.
We can just hope that we manage to choose suitable third-hand descriptions so that such biasing may be considered "good".

When still lacking direct first-hand experience, 
e.g. due to not having practiced enough to have sufficiently precise or fine graind clarity, 
I choose to use the following three criteria:
  c) compatibility with what is known about the phenomenology of the interpretive layers from points #6-10 above.
  d) convergence across traditions that have had no or little contact with each other, and
  e) internal consistency.

Note that I choose to downplay a little the role of "internal consistency," as internal consistency in the sense
we understand that today not been a strong criterion in some of the cultures that I am interested in.
(I will return to this later on, below, as going deeper into this requires comtemplative understanding that I cannot assume yet.)

### Summary of the first ontological and methodological assumptions

Continuing the numbering of #1–10 from above, I choose to assume the following points:

11. Other minds similar to my mind(s) do exist.

12. There exists some sort of a communication channel between those other minds and my mind(s).

13. That communication channel is typically experienced as the external world.

Note that this assumption does not exclude the possibility of the #12 communication channel
sometimes taking the form of other experiences, such as telepathy.  
It just records how we typically experience the inter-mind communciation in our culture.

14. Many of those other minds are willing to share their first-hand experiences as accurately as they can.

Herein the accuracy and structures are strongly limited and biased by their surrounding culture(s).

15. There has been and are cultures that have aimed to collect, corroborate and curate such reports.

This includes our current materialistic culture, which has aimed to collect, corroborate and curate
our experiences of the so-called material world, including ones from mundane everyday 
experiences to those purportedly gained through large-scale apparatuses such as the Large Hadron Collider.

This also includes the wisdom traditions selected for this ontology project, namely
Advaita Vedānta, Kashmir Shaivism (Trika Kaula), and Dzogchen.

16. None of our experience, including the low-level immediate affective response, is free from bias.

It is important to notice that from the ontological point of view even the
lowest automatic layers of our interpretative apparatus certainly have their own biases in
when it comes to representing the (materialistic) world accurately.  
For example, due to the (assumed) evolutionary pressures, even what our system
considers as immediate, instinctive good/neutral/bad is biased to keeping our
system alive and functioning and does not tell (almost) anything about the ontological status
of what causes the underlying perceptions.

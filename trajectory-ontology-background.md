# Background: How the trajectory ontology emerged

This document explains the reasoning path that led to the trajectory ontology described in the other files. The goal is to help readers—especially those coming from a conventional physicalist worldview—understand the conceptual steps without getting stuck at the same points where the discussion originally slowed down.

The ontology did not start as a rejection of physics or of the scientific worldview. Instead, it began with a simple observation: many spiritual and contemplative traditions describe human experience in ways that do not fit easily into a single fixed-history model of reality.

## Starting point: the conventional worldview

The dominant modern worldview assumes roughly the following:

- The universe follows a single consistent physical history.
- Each person occupies one continuous worldline within that shared history.
- Memory stores records of earlier events along that same history.
- Apparent inconsistencies in recollection are explained as errors in perception or memory.

For most everyday purposes this model works extremely well. The overwhelming majority of experiences are fully consistent with it.

However, the model is rarely questioned because it is implicitly assumed rather than explicitly examined.

## Observing anomalies in lived experience

The initial motivation for revisiting the model came from small but persistent anomalies in lived experience. These anomalies are typically subtle and involve discrepancies such as:

- remembered quantities differing from present observations,
- environmental details appearing inconsistent with earlier visits,
- locations or structures seeming different without clear signs of change.

However, this explanation is not logically required. It is simply the most common interpretation under the assumption that a single fixed shared history exists.

This observation opens a conceptual possibility: what if experience is not confined to a single history?

## Introducing the trajectory landscape

Instead of assuming a single linear timeline, reality is modeled as a landscape of possible histories. This idea is not foreign to modern physics; several interpretations of quantum mechanics and cosmology already consider multiple histories or branches.

Within such a landscape:

- each **history** represents one complete physical evolution of a world,
- each observer or physical system traces a **worldline** within a given history,
- nearby histories may differ only slightly,
- conscious experience might follow a **trajectory** that can move among nearby histories.

This does not require that all histories interact freely. Local coherence and continuity still impose strong constraints.

## From branching futures to lateral movement

Most discussions of multiple histories focus only on branching futures. In such models, different choices lead to different possible future paths.

However, once multiple histories exist, nothing in principle forbids movement between **nearby histories that also differ slightly in their past**. This is called *lateral movement*.

In this ontology:

- a **trajectory-family** represents a cluster of nearby compatible histories,
- a conscious system occupies a **locus of experience** within one of those histories,
- navigation may allow movement within the trajectory-family.

Large discontinuities remain unlikely because continuity of experience must be preserved.  It appears that emotinally intensive periods of life, such as deep retreates, quest-like journeys, rupture periods, transformation experiences, etc, seem to "allow" larger discontinuities and may therefore allow larger lateral movements.

## Reinterpreting memory

A key conceptual shift concerns memory.

Under strict physicalism, memory is often assumed to be stored entirely in brain structures. Yet neuroscience mainly demonstrates correlations between neural states and remembered content; it does not fully explain the nature of memory itself.

Within the trajectory ontology, memory is interpreted functionally as a **continuity interface**. Its role is to maintain coherent narrative experience across transitions rather than to serve as a perfect archive of an objective past.

This interpretation allows for minor discrepancies in remembered events without requiring either perfect recall or total unreliability.

## Connection with contemplative traditions

Once framed this way, several ideas from Buddhist and Kashmir Shaiva traditions become easier to interpret.

For example:

- **karma** can be understood as attractor structures that bias navigation within the trajectory landscape,
- **recognition** (pratyabhijñā) describes a loosening of limitations that expand accessible trajectories,
- **rigpa** or nondual awareness corresponds to experiential states where identification with a single narrative self disappers.

These traditions may be describing features of experiential navigation rather than making metaphysical claims about supernatural realms.

## From teleology to strucure and the role of enlightenment

The contemplative traditions often implicitly assume that life has a cosmic purpose and present enlightment as the purpose of life.

In this model, the situation changes. The universe just is, with structure. Purpose is a local experience, tied to the trajectory, never cosmic.

The role and understanding of enlightenment changes radically.
See [Reinterpretation of enlightenment](trajectory-ontology-working-note.md#reinterpretation-of-enlightenment).

## Why compassion becomes central

In this model, ethical principles are not merely moral rules but navigation heuristics.

Strong compassion (karuṇā) tends to guide experience toward trajectory-families characterized by reduced suffering and greater relational coherence.

Thus ethical transformation can be interpreted as movement toward more stable and less destructive attractors in the experiential landscape.

The role of compassion is very central in this model and not at all clear, yet.

## Remaining uncertainties

This ontology remains exploratory. Several questions remain open:

- What mechanisms might permit lateral movement between nearby histories?
- How large is the set of trajectories accessible to a given system?
- What roles do attention, emotional intensity, trauma integration, and insight play in navigation?

The purpose of this model is not to claim definitive answers but to provide a framework that allows these questions to be explored without prematurely restricting possibilities.

## Purpose of the model

The trajectory ontology serves three practical purposes:

1. To offer a conceptual framework that accommodates both scientific knowledge and contemplative experience.
2. To reinterpret traditional concepts such as karma and awakening in structural rather than supernatural terms.
3. To explore how human consciousness might navigate a landscape of experiential possibilities.

Whether or not lateral movement between histories ultimately proves to be physically real, the model already helps illuminate how patterns of perception, emotion, and action shape the worlds we experience.

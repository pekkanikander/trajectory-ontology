# Trajectory Ontology — On Enlightenment

## Overview

This document is a working note on the trajectory ontology, specifically on the concept of enlightenment.

## Purpose

This note summarises an earlier long discussion with LLM
on enlightenment, altered states, phenomenology, PSI, and metaphysical ontology.
It records the main distinctions, hypotheses, and open problems that emerged back then.
It is written as background material for the present development of **trajectory ontology**.
It does **not** attempt to be a final synthesis.

The most important result of the discussion was
a six-aspect phenomenological model of awakening-like states,
together with a structural distinction between:

- **structural disruptions** of the ordinary mind, and
- **qualitative modulations** of experience.

## Initial discussion

### 1. Starting point

The original discussion began from a comparative question:
many traditions appear to describe a family of experiences usually called
awakening, enlightenment, kenshō, rigpa-recognition, nibbāna, illumination, gnosis, and so on.
These traditions differ strongly in language, doctrine, and cosmology,
yet they seem to converge on certain phenomenological structures.

The initial comparative field included, among others:

- Advaita Vedānta
- Kashmir Shaivism / Trika Kaula
- Classical Yoga
- Theravāda Buddhism
- Zen
- Tibetan Dzogchen and Bön
- Gnostic and mystical Christianity
- Sufism
- Kabbalah
- Ancient mystery traditions
- modern transpersonal / non-dual / contemplative teachers

The working method became:

1. bracket doctrinal truth claims,
2. compare phenomenology,
3. use modern cognitive science and neuroscience as a structural interpretive layer,
4. only then ask what, if anything, follows ontologically.

### 2. The six phenomenological aspects

A central result of the discussion was that awakening-like states should not be treated as one single thing.
Instead, they seem to vary along at least six partly separable dimensions.

| Label   | Aspect                          | Short description                                        |
|---------|---------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------|
| **(a)** | **Self-model / identification** | Non-identification with a self, subject, or experiencer. |
| **(b)** | **Time-model**                  | Linear time or temporal narrative not present.           |
| **(c)** | **Body / boundaries model**     | Bodily boundaries or spatial boundedness not present.    |
| **(d)** | **Clarity**                     | Vividness / luminosity present.                          |
| **(e)** | **Stillness**                   | Quieting of thoughts and mental movements present.       |
| **(f)** | **Bliss**                       | Peace, bliss, equanimity, warmth present.                |

#### Key methodological point

These six aspects are **not** assumed always to occur together.

That was one of the main corrections made in the discussion.
Traditional and modern descriptions often bundle them together,
but actual reports suggest many combinations are possible.
We have a family of altered structural and qualitative configurations.

### 3. Two major groups: structural vs qualitative

A further result was the division of the six aspects into two groups.

#### 3.1 Structural aspects

These appear to concern the **constructive machinery** of ordinary experience:

- **(a) self-model**
- **(b) time-model**
- **(c) body/boundaries model**

These were treated as **generative or structural models** that ordinarily shape experience into something like:

> “I am this person, here, now, inside this body, moving in time from the past to the future.”

#### 3.2 Qualitative aspects

These appear to concern the **quality or tone** of experience once it is occurring:

- **(d) clarity**
- **(e) stillness**
- **(f) bliss**

These do not seem to define the existence of a self/world structure directly.
Rather, they describe how experience is toned or modulated.

#### Working conclusion

This split seems to be judged useful and plausible.
It also seems broadly consistent with modern altered-state taxonomies:

- self/time/body disturbances often form one class,
- perceptual, attentional, affective, and tranquility shifts form another.

This distinction was important because it prevented an overly romantic reading of “enlightenment.”

### 4. Cognitive-scientific interpretation of the structural aspects

The discussion then explored the first three aspects structurally.

#### 4.1 (a) Self-model / identification

The hypothesis is that
ordinary consciousness contains a usually continuous **identification process**, which
tacitly binds experience to an apparent experiencer.

Relevant modern frameworks discussed:

- Thomas Metzinger’s phenomenal self-model
- predictive-processing (PP) / generative self-modelling
- attention-schema approaches
- DMN-related self-referential processing

Working interpretation:

- ordinary consciousness constructs a transparent sense of “I” or “me”,
- in some altered states this self-binding weakens or collapses,
- then experience may remain, but no longer appears as “owned” by an experiencer.

This was treated as a primary candidate for aspect **(a)**.

#### 4.2 (b) Time-model / linear temporality

The hypothesis is that the ordinary sense of linear time is also an active construction.

Relevant modern frameworks discussed:

- temporal prediction and predictive-processing accounts
- internal clock / distributed time-perception models
- mental time travel and narrative temporal continuity
- DMN-related autobiographical time structuring

Working interpretation:

- ordinary consciousness imposes temporal succession, continuity, and narrative order,
- altered states may weaken or suspend this temporal structuring,
- leading to timelessness, eternal present, looping, or loss of narrative sequence.

This was treated as a primary candidate for aspect **(b)**.

#### 4.3 (c) Body / boundaries model

The hypothesis is that the ordinary sense of being a bounded human organism in space is also actively constructed.

Relevant modern frameworks discussed:

- body schema and body image
- TPJ-related self-location and embodiment
- multisensory integration
- predictive body-modelling

Working interpretation:

- ordinary consciousness continually models bodily limits and self/world boundaries,
- altered states can weaken this construction,
- producing boundlessness, merging, or other changes in the felt scale of selfhood.

This was treated as a primary candidate for aspect **(c)**.

### 5. Important observation: the three structural aspects can vary independently

A central and personally important result of the discussion was that
the three structural aspects do **not** necessarily collapse together.

A specific personal experiential example discussed in detail was a state approximately characterised as:

- strong **(a)** non-identification,
- mild or moderate **(b)** weakening of linear time,
- little or no **(c)** loss of bodily boundaries,
- strong **(d)** clarity,
- little **(e)** stillness,
- little **(f)** bliss.

This was compared especially to Dzogchen **trekchö-like** episodes.

That mattered because many traditional presentations tend to imply that
selflessness, timelessness, boundarylessness, clarity, silence, and bliss arrive as one package.
The discussion concluded that this is phenomenologically misleading.

Instead, different altered states seem to involve different combinations of these six aspects.

### 6. Memory, narrative self, and the loss of autobiographical integration

The discussion also explored what happens to memory when aspect **(a)** becomes strong.

A working hypothesis was:

- when the self-model is absent or greatly weakened,
- experience may fail to bind itself into ordinary autobiographical narrative memory,
- therefore some selfless states may be hard to remember or narrate afterward.

This was linked to:

- autobiographical memory systems,
- narrative self-construction,
- phenomenological reports from Buddhist, non-dual, mystical, and psychedelic contexts.

The important distinction was between:

- **experience occurring**, and
- **that experience being narratively integrated later**.

This helped explain why some states may feel perfectly clear while occurring, yet later remain hard to describe.

### 7. Common phenomenological descriptions across traditions

The discussion noted recurring cross-traditional descriptors, including:

- no separate self,
- timelessness,
- boundlessness,
- extraordinary clarity or luminosity,
- silence or stillness,
- peace, bliss, or joy,
- unity or non-duality.

However, the discussion repeatedly corrected against treating these as a single indivisible unit.
They are better understood as a recurring **cluster**, with different traditions emphasizing different subsets.

## Relation to PSI and a critique of physicalism

A second major line of discussion introduced PSI-related evidence and broader non-physicalist debates.

The line of thought was roughly this:

1. robust PSI-type evidence, if accepted at face value, strongly pressures physicalism,
2. many traditions also describe states where ordinary mind-structure collapses,
3. therefore ordinary waking consciousness is doubly decentered:
   - by anomalies that exceed standard physicalist models,
   - and by contemplative states that expose the constructedness of ordinary mind.

This produced an important caution:

> one must not infer ontology too quickly from ordinary consciousness, nor too quickly from “pure” altered phenomenology.

In particular, the discussion resisted the easy move:

- “because pure states feel like pure consciousness, therefore the basis of reality simply is universal consciousness.”

This was treated as plausible but under-justified.

## Provisional metaphysical conclusions

A modest and cautious conclusion was preferred.

### What seemed justified

- physicalism should be rejected or at least radically weakened,
- ordinary self/time/boundary structures are constructed,
- altered states reveal this constructedness,
- traditions preserve phenomenological maps of these shifts,
- many traditions develop cosmologies in response to such states.

### What seemed not justified

- that the felt character of “pure” states directly reveals ultimate ontology,
- that one can straightforwardly infer a cosmic or universal consciousness from these states,
- that one specific tradition’s metaphysical interpretation should be adopted as final.

### Best tentative position

The discussion leaned toward something like:

- there may be a more fundamental ground or field,
- but if so, our descriptions of it are likely still mediated by human phenomenology,
- therefore traditional metaphysical ontologies are best treated initially as **mythopoetic phenomenological cosmologies**, not as final ontological descriptions.

This was an important freeing move.

## Practical consequences: possible to pursue awakening without formal refuge

A further important discussion concerned the pragmatic consequence of all this.

A working conclusion was reached that:

- one need not commit strictly to any one tradition in order to explore or stabilise awakening-like states,
- traditions are phenomenologically rich but also mythopoetically packaged,
- therefore it may be possible to respectfully learn from them without taking formal refuge in their full cosmological or institutional forms.

This came with strong cautions:

- going fully solo greatly increases risks of self-deception,
- analytical clarity becomes more necessary, not less,
- peers, mirrors, and ethical vigilance become crucial,
- stabilised freedom does not automatically purify shadow or guarantee compassion.

This produced an important separation:

- **collapse of the default stack** is not the same thing as ethical purification,
- enlightenment-as-freedom is not identical with enlightenment-as-full-integration.

## Major unresolved issues: trauma, shadow, and purification

A later part of the discussion began to connect:

- developmental trauma,
- intergenerational trauma,
- societal trauma,
- PSI-like phenomena,
- body-level and energy-level processing,
- and the possibility that radical states of freedom do not themselves heal the shadow.

This part remained incomplete and was judged to require a separate thread.

Still, one key result was clear:

> the collapse of the default stack does not by itself metabolise trauma, shadow, or ethical distortion.

This remains a major open problem for any serious ontology of enlightenment.

## Summary conclusions

### On phenomenology

The traditions seem broadly right about the **phenomenology** of awakened and awakening-like states.

Their descriptions agree to a significant degree:

- with each other,
- with modern empirical data,
- and with modern cognitive/neuroscientific structural hypotheses.

### On cosmology

The traditions also seem to converge on a family of **mythopoetic cosmologies** in which the ground of reality is described as some combination of:

- empty,
- luminous,
- and dynamically responsive / compassionate / loving.

But it remains unclear whether these cosmologies describe reality as such, or phenomenological structures encountered when the ordinary stack collapses.

### On ontology

It remains unclear whether anything final can be said about the “truer” ontology behind all this.

A modest conclusion from the discussion was:

- perhaps the best we can do, at least for now, is to describe how the universe appears and feels from the fullest clarified range of human phenomenology,
- while remaining cautious about inflating such phenomenology into final ontology.

This restraint was treated not as defeat, but as a methodological virtue.

## Relevance for trajectory ontology

From the perspective of **trajectory ontology**, the most important inheritance from the earlier discussion is not any final metaphysical claim, but the following toolkit:

### A phenomenological grammar

The six aspects provide a compact grammar for discussing awakening-like states without collapsing them into one category.

### A structural model of ordinary mind

The first three aspects in particular suggest that ordinary human consciousness is scaffolded by at least three major constructive systems:

- self / identification,
- linear time,
- body / boundary localisation.

This is highly relevant for any ontology that treats lived existence as structured, developmental, or trajectory-like.

### A methodological caution

One must distinguish carefully between:

- phenomenology,
- cognitive structure,
- mythopoetic cosmology,
- and ontology.

The earlier discussion repeatedly showed how easily these levels become entangled.

### A practical-existential consequence

One may possibly walk a path of serious transformation without strict subordination to any one tradition, but only at the price of:

- greater epistemic burden,
- greater need for honesty and peers,
- and greater exposure to shadow distortions.

## Open questions left unresolved

The earlier discussion did **not** resolve the following:

1. What is the best ontology compatible with:
   - PSI evidence,
   - structural cognitive models,
   - and contemplative phenomenology?
2. Is there any justified move from “pure state phenomenology” to a claim about the basis of reality?
3. How should trauma, shadow, and ethical formation be integrated with structural freedom?
4. Is there a better alternative to traditional ground-ontologies such as gzhi, Paramaśiva, Brahman, etc.?
5. How should one relate personally and publicly to traditions while declining full doctrinal refuge?

These questions were left open.

# Appendices: Specific traditions

## 1. Dzogchen and Bön: structural rendering in modern terms

The discussion then examined Dzogchen/Bön terminology through the new framework.

### 1.1 Key terms

- **gzhi** = ground / basis
- **rigpa** = pristine awareness / recognition
- triad often rendered as:
  - **ngo bo** = essence-aspect (emptiness)
  - **rang bzhin** = nature-aspect (luminosity / spontaneous display)
  - **thugs rje** = energy / compassion / dynamic responsiveness

### 1.2 Working modern rendering

The discussion proposed that ordinary mind (**sems**) corresponds broadly to the full operation of the default stack, while **rigpa-recognition** corresponds to states in which some or all of the default stack weakens and a more direct, model-light mode of knowing becomes available.

A careful distinction was made:

- **gzhi** is not simply “a state”,
- **rigpa** is not just another altered state among others,
- but phenomenological episodes may still provide clues as to why these traditions describe the ground as empty, luminous, and dynamically responsive.

### 1.3 Connection to the six aspects

A tentative mapping was explored:

- emptiness ↔ especially **(a)** and **(c)**, perhaps also **(b)** and **(e)**,
- luminosity ↔ especially **(d)**,
- thugs rje ↔ especially **(f)**, though not reducible to bliss.

The discussion stressed that these were heuristic correspondences, not strict equivalences.

## 2. Trika Kaula: structural rendering in modern terms

The discussion then made a similar move with Trika Kaula.

### 2.1 Key terms

- **Paramaśiva / Parashiva / Anuttara** = ultimate reality
- **prakāśa** = luminosity / shining awareness
- **vimarśa** = reflexive self-knowing
- **spanda** = pulsation / dynamic vibration

Trika’s layered psychology and cosmology were also discussed through:

- the **kañcukas** (limitations)
- the **malas** (impurities / contractions)

### 2.2 Working modern rendering

This was interpreted as another highly structured model of how infinite or unconstrained awareness becomes contracted into ordinary human finitude.

The comparison with the six-aspect model suggested:

- some Trika structures map naturally to self-contraction,
- others to temporal contraction,
- others to boundedness, limited agency, limited knowing, and affective incompleteness.

### 2.3 Comparison to Dzogchen

The discussion found strong family resemblance between Dzogchen and Trika, but also important differences:

- Dzogchen tends to foreground **emptiness** first,
- Trika tends to foreground **luminosity and reflexive dynamism** first.

A tentative mapping explored was:

- Dzogchen **ka dag** ↔ Trika **prakāśa**, but imperfectly,
- Dzogchen **lhun grub** ↔ Trika **prakāśa + vimarśa**, more strongly,
- Dzogchen **thugs rje** ↔ Trika **spanda**, moderately strongly.

Again, these were treated as useful but non-isomorphic correspondences.

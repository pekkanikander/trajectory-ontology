# Glossary

A **block universe** in physics and philosophy usually means a four-dimensional spacetime in which past, present, and future are all equally real. In this ontology the term is used more loosely for a block-style reality containing multiple possible histories. The ontology therefore extends the standard notion toward a multiverse of histories rather than using the term in its narrowest technical sense.

A **history** is one complete physical evolution of a world across spacetime, or an idealized reconstruction of such an evolution from a sufficiently coherent web of records and worldlines. In this ontology, histories are useful large-scale units for describing possible worlds, even if they ultimately turn out not to be fully fundamental or sharply separated everywhere.

A **worldline** is the path of a particular physical system or observer through spacetime, as used in relativity. A worldline is local to that system; it is not the entire history of a world.

A **timeline** is a conventional or narrative description of events ordered along a single linear history. It is a looser and more human-level term than *worldline*.

A **trajectory** is the path of experienced states taken by a conscious system through the landscape of possible experiential configurations. In this ontology, a trajectory is not necessarily tied to a single fixed history but may involve movement within a family of nearby histories.

A **thread** is an informal term for a single history when discussing nearby alternative histories. It is not a technical physics term.

A **locus of experience** denotes the instantaneous experiential configuration of a conscious system. More conventionally, it denotes what human experiences "right now," or, slightly more strictly speaking, what a conscious system experiences at a given "point" in the block universe.

An **experiential bubble** is the region of reality effectively coupled to a locus of experience: the domain within which interactions, records, and environmental states are relevant enough to sustain that locus's perceptual, narrative, and practical coherence. It need not be a sharply bounded spatial region; it is better understood as a boundary of effective coupling. This is a _central work-in-progress concept_ in this ontology, because the nature of experiential bubbles may determine how local coherence, decoherence, and continuity of experience are structured.

**Lateral movement** refers to an experiential transition between nearby histories that differ slightly in past or environmental details while preserving local experiential continuity. Such movement would shift a locus of experience between neighbouring trajectories within a trajectory-family. From the conventional point of view, some lateral movements may be understood as choosing between possible futures while others may involve also "changing" the past.

A **trajectory-family** is a set of nearby compatible histories accessible to a conscious system. Each history contains many physical worldlines. In this ontology, the trajectory-family concerns how experience may move between large-scale coherent possibilities, not how a single object moves through spacetime within one history.

**Navigation** denotes purported human ability to move laterally within a trajectory-family. From the conventional point of view, this includes actions ranging from simple choices of what to do next to much larger, "life changing" events.

An **attractor** is a stable pattern of behaviour, perception, and experience toward which a system repeatedly returns. In the trajectory landscape, attractors correspond to regions of experiential space that strongly influence navigation.

**Continuity** refers to the preservation of coherent experience across transitions. It typically includes identity continuity, memory compatibility, and sufficient environmental consistency.

**Local coherence** means that the immediate environment and internal state of the system, i.e. the experiential bubble of the system, remain physically and narratively consistent after a transition, even if the broader history differs slightly.

**Memory compatibility** means that memories are compatible enough with the experienced now. **Personal memory compatibility** means that any discrepancies between the experienced now and the memories are small enough that the person in question can accept them. **Group memory compatibility** means that the differences between remembered histories are small enough that the group can accept them.

**Karma** is interpreted here as patterned tendencies that bias navigation through the trajectory landscape. Rather than deterministic fate, karma functions as an attractor structure shaping which trajectories are most accessible.

**Compassion** (karuṇā) is treated as a navigation principle that biases movement toward trajectories with reduced suffering and increased relational coherence.

**Recognition** refers to the experiential realisation that individual consciousness is not separate from the larger field of awareness. In Kashmir Shaiva terminology this is *pratyabhijñā*, the recognition of one's identity with the universal consciousness.

**Contraction** describes the limiting processes that narrow the range of accessible experiential states. **Expansion** refers to the loosening of those limits, allowing access to a wider region of the trajectory landscape.

**Rigpa** is the direct nondual awareness described in Dzogchen traditions, characterised by clarity, openness, and absence of identification with a separate self.  In other traditions, _similar_ kinds of awareness are denoted by e.g. śūnyatā and samma-samādhi.

The **memory interface** is the functional role of memory in maintaining continuity of experience. In this model memory may act as an interface ensuring narrative coherence rather than as a perfect archival record of past events.

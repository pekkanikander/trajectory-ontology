# The Mandela Effect in the Trajectory Ontology

## 1. The phenomenon

The term "Mandela Effect" was coined by Fiona Broome around 2009–2010, naming the observation that large numbers of people share memories of Nelson Mandela having died in prison in the 1980s, despite his having lived until 2013. The phenomenon extends well beyond this instance: cross-person convergence on specific false versions of commercial logos, film dialogue, product names, and biographical facts has been documented in dozens of cases. The misremembered versions are consistent — not randomly distributed across plausible alternatives — and are reported with subjective confidence comparable to, or exceeding, that for accurate memories.

From a standard single-timeline physicalist standpoint, the Mandela Effect is a collective cognitive failure requiring explanation. From the trajectory ontology, it is something more interesting: expected evidence that members of a present-moment community may have arrived there from slightly different threads of a trajectory-family, carrying memories that were accurate to their respective histories.

---

## 2. The mainstream cognitive account

Memory science has, over several decades, established that human memory is reconstructive rather than reproductive. The following mechanisms are well-evidenced and explain a substantial portion of all false memory phenomena, Mandela Effect instances included.

**Reconstructive memory.** Bartlett (1932) showed that recall of stories is systematically distorted toward the listener's schemas and expectations. What is retrieved is a plausible reconstruction shaped by prior knowledge, not a literal replay of encoded content.

**The misinformation effect.** Loftus and Palmer (1974) demonstrated that post-event verbal framing alters the content of retrieved memories. Subsequent work showed that entirely false autobiographical memories — for events that did not occur — can be implanted with moderate reliability. The misinformation effect predicts that people who encounter false versions of logos, quotes, or facts will subsequently misattribute those versions to direct past experience.

**DRM false memories.** The Deese–Roediger–McDermott paradigm (Roediger and McDermott 1995) shows that exposure to semantically related word lists reliably produces confident false recognition of critical lures — words not presented but implied by the set. This demonstrates that schema-driven semantic completion is a pervasive and largely automatic feature of memory encoding. Iconic visual stimuli such as commercial logos are prime candidates for gist-dominated, schema-completed encoding.

**Source monitoring failures.** Johnson, Hashtroudi, and Lindsay (1993) formalised source monitoring theory: memory errors arise when the origin of a mental representation — perceived, imagined, read, or encountered via social transmission — is misattributed. A Mandela Effect instance is structurally a source monitoring error: an image or fact encountered in one context (a joke, an internet discussion, a misread caption) is later attributed to direct past experience.

**Fuzzy trace theory.** Brainerd and Reyna (2002) distinguish verbatim traces from gist traces. Gist-based retrieval is schema-sensitive and generates coherent but potentially inaccurate outputs. Widely-encountered cultural artefacts — logos, slogans, character names — are encoded primarily via gist, making them especially susceptible to prototype-completion errors.

**Memory conformity.** Gabbert, Memon, and Allan (2003) showed that witnesses discussing a shared event adopt each other's false memories via social transmission. Once a Mandela Effect instance achieves circulation, social contagion can propagate the shared misremembering rapidly and extensively, creating an apparent cross-person consistency without any need for a common prior experience.

These mechanisms are well-supported and non-trivial. Any trajectory-ontology account must acknowledge their genuine scope. The claim is not that they are wrong, but that they are insufficient.

## 3. Structural limits of the standard account

Several features of documented Mandela Effect instances resist clean reduction to the mechanisms above.

**Cross-person convergence on a single specific alternative.** Schema completion and source monitoring errors should distribute across the space of plausible alternatives in proportion to their semantic or visual salience. Mandela Effect instances systematically do not. They cluster on *one specific wrong version* rather than distributing across the plausible variant space. The question "where did the shared misinformation come from in the first place?" is typically answered circularly: the false memory spread because it was already widespread. This is underdetermination, not explanation.

**Vividness and confidence of false memories.** Prasad and Bainbridge (2022) conducted controlled experiments on ten visual Mandela Effect stimuli. Participants were asked to draw the stimuli from memory. False versions — the misremembered images — were drawn with significantly more detail and rated with higher confidence than correct versions. This inverts the standard pattern: false memories are normally less vivid and less detailed than accurate ones, and confidence is a known unreliable but generally positive correlate of accuracy. The visual Mandela Effect memories behave as though the false version is *better* encoded than the true one.

**Temporal robustness after disconfirmation.** For schema-completion errors, exposure to the correct version normally produces rapid revision. Some Mandela Effect instances persist in individuals who have been presented with repeated corrections and verifiable evidence. This is not characteristic of confabulation or schema-prototype substitution.

**Cross-cultural instances.** Social contagion is most plausible within shared media environments. Where the same misremembering appears across linguistically and culturally distinct populations with no common media source, the contagion explanation requires an implausibly global propagation mechanism.

None of these anomalies falsifies the cognitive account on its own. Together, they establish underdetermination: the data are consistent with cognitive-failure explanations but are, in key respects, better accommodated by the trajectory ontology.

## 4. Trajectory ontology reframing

The trajectory ontology offers not a replacement of the cognitive mechanisms above but a recontextualisation of the frame within which they operate.

**Memory as continuity interface.** The ontology treats memory not as archival storage but as a *continuity interface*: its functional role is to maintain narrative and experiential coherence as a locus of experience moves through trajectory space (see Glossary). Under this view, memory is calibrated to sustain coherence within the *current* thread of the trajectory-family, not to faithfully archive a fixed past. After a lateral movement, memories may be slightly misaligned with the currently observable record — not because they are "wrong" in an archival sense, but because they were accurate to a prior history. The manifest content of the memory is not an encoding error; it is a trace of another thread.

**Group memory compatibility.** The Glossary distinguishes *personal memory compatibility* — discrepancies small enough that the individual can accept them — from *group memory compatibility* — differences between remembered histories small enough that the group can accept them collectively. Lateral movements that preserve personal continuity do not simultaneously guarantee group continuity. Different members of a shared present community may have arrived from slightly different nearby threads, carrying slightly different memories. The Mandela Effect is precisely what group memory *incompatibility* looks like from within a single shared present: a community whose members share a now but whose remembered histories are subtly, consistently different.

**Peripheral details and the experiential bubble boundary.** The structural constraint on lateral movement requires local coherence within the experiential bubble — not global coherence across all background detail. Peripheral elements — commercial logos, film dialogue, biographical details of distant public figures — fall near the outer edge of an individual's experiential bubble. They are exactly the elements where cross-thread variation is most likely and hardest to detect within a single thread. The category of things subject to Mandela Effect misremembering is structurally predicted by the ontology: it is the category of details that lie at or beyond the effective coupling boundary of ordinary experience.

**Single-alternative convergence as trajectory-family topology.** The cross-person convergence on a single specific false version has a natural interpretation in the trajectory ontology. If the "false" version is the version present in a nearby, highly populated thread of the trajectory-family, lateral movement toward that thread — by many individuals, independently — would produce consistent convergence on the same specific memory. The systematic character of the Mandela Effect reflects the *topology* of the trajectory-family: some threads are more populated, more accessible, and more gravitationally present than others. The attractor structure of the trajectory landscape shapes which specific alternative is remembered, rather than distributing errors randomly across the plausible variant space.

## 5. Relation to adjacent phenomena

The Mandela Effect is not isolated within the trajectory ontology. It is continuous with other anomalous-memory phenomena the framework already addresses.

**Past-life memories in children.** Tucker (2005) and Stevenson (1997) document cases in which young children report detailed, verifiable memories of lives in different bodies, sometimes with correspondences to historical records. The standard account dismisses these as confabulation or coaching. Under the trajectory ontology they represent lateral movement across more distant threads, carrying memories that belong to a genuinely different history. The distance from the Mandela Effect to these cases is a matter of degree — both involve memories that are accurate to a thread other than the currently shared one.

**NDE perception anomalies.** Van Lommel et al. (2001) documented perception of verifiable events at a distance from the patient's body during clinical absence of consciousness. These cannot arise from standard memory encoding in the experiencing subject. They are interpretable as perception occurring outside the normal boundaries of the experiential bubble — relevant here because they establish empirically that the boundary of the experiential bubble is not fixed and can, under extreme conditions, shift substantially.

**Neuropsychological confabulation.** Kopelman (1987) distinguishes two types of confabulation: provoked (arising under direct memory testing) and spontaneous (arising unprompted in ordinary discourse). Both demonstrate the constructive, inferential character of memory at the neurological level. This is entirely compatible with the trajectory ontology, which does not deny the neural correlates of memory but treats them as interface-level phenomena. The confabulation literature makes explicit how fragile and reconstructively active the interface is — precisely what the trajectory ontology's "memory as interface" framing predicts would be the case.

**Collective memory as structured field.** The sociology of collective memory (Halbwachs 1950; Nora 1989) documents how communities maintain stable, sometimes counterfactual, memories as part of group identity and social practice. Collective memory is not the aggregate of individual recollections; it has structural properties independent of any single member. Halbwachs argued that all individual memory is inherently social in its formation and maintenance. The trajectory ontology offers a potential physical interpretation of this structural independence: collective memory may partially reflect the shared trajectory-family from which a community's members have navigated to the present, not merely social consensus imposed on individual encoding.

## 6. Differential predictions

| Feature | Standard cognitive account | Trajectory ontology |
|---|---|---|
| Origin of cross-person convergence | Social contagion of a prototype error | Shared thread of the trajectory-family |
| Vividness of Mandela Effect memories | Should be lower than accurate memories | May be higher if they are traces of a prior history |
| Distribution of errors | Spread across plausible-variant space | Clustered sharply on a single nearby-thread version |
| Persistence after correction | Should decrease with repeated disconfirmation | May persist if the history-trace is deeply interface-constitutive |
| Category of susceptible details | Any frequently misprocessed stimulus | Preferentially peripheral details near experiential bubble boundary |
| Individual variation in susceptibility | Inversely correlated with source monitoring ability and critical thinking | Related to pattern and frequency of lateral movement |

The most diagnostically useful prediction is the *distribution* one. Schema-completion errors should spread across the semantically or visually plausible variant space in proportion to prototype strength. Trajectory-family topology predicts sharp clustering on a single specific alternative regardless of its objective prototype strength. Prasad and Bainbridge's (2022) data are consistent with the latter; the standard account predicts the former but has not produced a satisfying explanation of why thousands of people converge on the *same* wrong image rather than diverse plausible variants.

## 7. Open questions

- Does Mandela Effect susceptibility correlate with variables predicted by the trajectory ontology — for instance, measures of contemplative depth, trauma load, or self-reported anomalous memory in other domains?
- Can the topology of trajectory-family neighbourhood be partially inferred from the statistical structure of *which* peripheral details are and are not subject to cross-person misremembering?
- Do cross-cultural instances of the same Mandela Effect misremembering (where social contagion is less plausible) show the same convergence pattern as within a shared media environment?
- What is the functional relationship between the experiential bubble boundary and the empirical category of details subject to Mandela Effect misremembering? Can the bubble boundary be operationalised well enough to generate testable predictions?
- If the false version is a trace of a prior thread, is there any detectable asymmetry in the phenomenology of correction — i.e., do individuals report the correct version as somehow *stranger* or less natural than the false version?

---

## 8. Summary

The Mandela Effect, in standard cognitive science, is a tractable case of collective false memory explicable by reconstructive encoding, schema completion, source monitoring error, fuzzy-trace retrieval, and social contagion. These mechanisms handle much of the data and should not be dismissed.

The trajectory ontology offers a structurally different framing: cross-person memory inconsistency about peripheral details is *expected*, not anomalous, when members of a shared present community have navigated there from a spread of nearby but distinct threads in a trajectory-family. The most striking empirical anomaly — that Mandela Effect false memories are more vivid and detailed than correct memories — is better explained by the trajectory ontology: what is vivid is what was accurately encoded in a prior history, not a degraded reconstruction. The systematic convergence on a single specific alternative reflects the topology of the trajectory-family rather than the random distribution of confabulation errors.

The two accounts are underdetermined by existing data. The trajectory ontology does not require abandoning the cognitive mechanisms; it reframes their operation within a broader ontological context in which the "false" memories may, in at least some cases, be accurate traces of other threads of the trajectory-family, carried through a lateral movement into a present that differs in those peripheral details.

# Objections and misunderstandings

## Common objections

The following objections are often raised in discussions related to this ontology.

**Objection:** Reports of unsual ("impossible") events or miracles are unreliable and should be ignored.

**Refutation:** The preceived impossibility of such events is a cultural bias, growing from the background of materialism.

Across all human cultures, reports of some sorts of "miracles" are pervasive.
Furthermore, there are quite a lot of data about some sorts of unusual events, such as NDE perceptions.
Hence, in a honest ontology, there must some ontological explanation for what is going on there.
The standard view, where such events cannot take place in the physical reality and
therefore are necessarily some kind of mental illusions or even group psychosis,
is fundamentally based on the standard assumption of what physical reality is and therefore
what is possible and what is impossible.

In this ontology, we take careful steps away from the standard physicalist assumptions,
thereby opening venues for new potential explanations
while at the same time applying a strict epistemic discipline.

In other words, we do want to open for discussion the possibility that
some of the so-called miracles and some unsual events are real and do have a physical correlate.
More importantly, though, we want to open up the discussion to the possibility that
some portion of the cases where people remember the same events differently or
where people feel that the physical reality is different now than what it was
are actually genuine and indicate lateral movements instead of memory failures.

We do not want to claim that the human memory
were not fallible in the standard sense, i.e. that the research results
indicating the fallibility were false as such.
At the same time, we want to be open to other datasets,
such as well documented extrasensory perception during NDE events
and the Virginia University Division of Perceptual Studies
Children Who Report Memories of Past Lives dataset.

The question is whether these datasets describe some aspects of reality that
are better explained by this ontology than the standard physicalist ontology.

This approach can be compared to any other situation,
where the anomalies in the datasets have habitually been explained away for decades,
typically as measurement errors or otherwise unreliable research,
but once accepted, have led to new science.

**Objection:** The potential explanations opening here, such as lateral movements, are not parsimonious.

**Refutation:** Maybe so, but also this objection appears to be a mental bias.

Familiar mental constructions appear to be more parsimonius than non-familiar ones,
due to the limitations of human memory and learning.
Hence, what appears first a clearly less parsimonius explanation may later turn out to be clearly more parsimonius.

This same argument applies also to LLMs, whose learning set includes large number of familiar constructions
while containing none or only a few instances of the new construction.
Hence, due to the nature of the LLM neural network, the familiar constructions are represented
with less effort and appear more "naturally" (with higher propability) than new constructions.

**Objection:** None of this can be verified objectively.

**Refutation:** Maybe so, if all non-conscious artefacts exist only within a single shared history.
In that case, there are no objective means of verifying lateral movements.
The only possibility is to study how humans narrate their experience.
Hence, objecting to this ontology on objectivity basis is a moot point.

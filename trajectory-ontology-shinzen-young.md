# Trajectory Ontology — Shinzen Young: Phenomenological Parallels

## Overview

This note examines the work of Shinzen Young — in particular *The Science of Enlightenment*
[@Young2016ScienceEnlightenment; @Young1997ScienceEnlightenmentAudio] — in relation to the trajectory
ontology. The aim is not a comprehensive survey of Young's work, but a structural comparison of his
phenomenological framework with the core commitments of this project.

## Young's project and its direction

Young's aim is to construct a tradition-stripped, systematised account of contemplative experience
that can engage with science on its own terms. He approaches this from a starting point of extensive
samādhi experience — principally in Zen and Theravāda lineages — and works outward toward scientific
and information-theoretic language. His primary contributions relevant here are:

- A decomposition of meditative experience into constituent sensory and mental modalities (vision,
  sound, body sensation, internal images, internal talk, emotion)
- A systematic account of how equanimity — as distinct from detachment — functions as the operative
  skill in practice
- A mathematically flavoured formula: *suffering = pain × resistance*, where zero equanimity
  (resistance = 1) maximises suffering, and perfect equanimity (resistance → 0) approaches zero
  suffering regardless of pain intensity
- An emphasis on the **gone moment** — the moment of complete cessation of a sensory or mental
  event — as phenomenologically primary and revelatory

His direction of travel is the inverse of this project's. Trajectory ontology moves from physics and
block-universe metaphysics inward toward experience; Young moves from phenomenological depth outward
toward physics. If both are correct, convergence is expected.

## Arising and passing: simultaneous expansion and contraction

Young makes a structural claim about the phenomenology of arising and passing that goes beyond
standard impermanence teaching. When observed with sufficient precision:

- **Arising** is not a simple emergence from nothing. It exhibits two simultaneous qualities: an
  **expanding** movement (something opens, spreads, intensifies outward) and a **contracting**
  movement (something localises, focuses, concentrates). Both occur together.
- **Passing** mirrors this: dissolution likewise involves simultaneous expansion (a releasing back
  into the field) and contraction (a compression of the specific configuration) — until both
  movements reach zero.
- **Gone** is the completion of this dual movement: not a negative result of subtraction but a
  positive state. Young describes it as having a quality of fullness or completeness.

This claim has three structural resonances within the trajectory ontology.

### The Whiteheadian mapping

Whitehead's account of an actual occasion in *Process and Reality* [@Whitehead1978ProcessReality]
involves two simultaneous movements: **physical prehension** (the new occasion inherits from prior
occasions — the determinate past contracts into the present configuration) and **conceptual
prehension** (the occasion integrates possibility, opens outward toward what it might become). These
are not sequential phases but aspects of a single event.

Young's simultaneous expansion and contraction in arising is the phenomenological interior of
Whiteheadian prehension as observed from inside. Passing is the occasion reaching its
**satisfaction** — both movements completing — and Gone is the moment of complete satisfaction
before the next occasion's prehension begins. The fullness of Gone is Whiteheadian satisfaction:
full because complete, not because anything is added.

### The QFT mapping

In quantum field theory, any field excitation involves two simultaneous aspects at the level of the
experiential bubble:

- **Field expansion**: the influence of the excitation propagates outward; entanglement extends.
- **Local decoherence**: the excitation concentrates into a definite configuration within the bubble
  — contraction into a branch.

Neither movement precedes the other; they are aspects of one event. The arising of a new locus of
experience, in the trajectory ontology's framework, should exhibit exactly this structure. Young is
detecting, phenomenologically from the inside, what decoherence-plus-field-expansion looks like from
the perspective of the system undergoing it.

### The trajectory-family mapping

At the level of the trajectory landscape: arising involves both **selection** (contraction — one
configuration crystallises from among nearby possibilities) and **opening** (expansion — the field
of possible continuations extends). Passing involves both **release** (expansion — the current
configuration dissolves back toward the undifferentiated field) and **compression** (contraction —
the specific pattern completes and closes). The Gone moment is where the experiential bubble
momentarily touches the unpartitioned trajectory-family before the next locus crystallises.

## The fullness of Gone as contact with the One

In the trajectory ontology's monism, the One is the undifferentiated block-universe field from
which all trajectories are selected. It is not empty but encompasses the entire landscape of
possibility — every trajectory, every attractor, every locus. In Kashmir Shaiva terms it is
Paramaśiva prior to the *spanda* of individual contraction; in Dzogchen it is the primordial *gzhi*,
described as luminous and self-perfected.

Young's report of Gone as full rather than empty is the phenomenological signature of a locus of
experience becoming briefly transparent to this background. The fullness is not content added to the
moment but the fullness of the ground that was always already there, momentarily unobscured.

This gives a more precise account of rigpa in trajectory-ontology terms. Rigpa — stabilised
recognition of the ground — is not a new state entered but the stabilisation of the openness that
is always present at the gone moment: the experiential bubble held consistently in its most
permeable configuration. The "recognition" in *pratyabhijñā* (Kashmir Shaivism) and the
corresponding Dzogchen pointing-out are then not cognitive acts of inference but structural
conditions: the locus of experience no longer closing back into a particular attractor after each
arising-passing cycle.

## Structural parallels: summary

| Young's term | Trajectory ontology correlate |
|---|---|
| Gone moment | Boundary between successive loci; maximal openness to the trajectory-family |
| Fullness of Gone | Contact with the One; undifferentiated block-universe background |
| *Suffering = pain × resistance* | Trajectory-lock as attractor depth; resistance = curvature of the basin |
| Equanimity (not detachment) | Rigpa as recognition without trajectory-lock |
| Simultaneous expansion + contraction in arising | Whiteheadian physical + conceptual prehension; QFT decoherence + field expansion |
| Stabilised attention to gone | Structural condition for lateral mobility; rigpa as sustained permeability |

## Navigational implication

If the gone moment is the point of maximum openness to the trajectory-family, then practice that
cultivates sustained attention to gone is — structurally — practice that keeps the experiential
bubble at its most laterally permeable. This provides a mechanism within the trajectory ontology for
why contemplative practice directed at the gone moment might shift what trajectories are accessible,
rather than merely changing one's attitude toward a fixed trajectory. Young's claim that sustained
equanimity changes what arises (not just how one relates to what arises) is consistent with this.

This remains speculative as a causal claim. But the structural alignment between Young's
phenomenology, the Whiteheadian prehension model, the QFT dual-movement picture, and the
trajectory-family account is sufficiently close to warrant treating them as convergent descriptions
of the same underlying structure approached from complementary directions.
